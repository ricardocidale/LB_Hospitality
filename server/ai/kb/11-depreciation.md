Depreciation, Taxes, and Cost Basis

## Depreciation Method

Hotels are classified as nonresidential real property under IRC §168(e)(2)(A). The IRS-mandated depreciation period is 39 years using straight-line depreciation under the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS). This is different from residential rental property (apartments, houses), which uses 27.5 years. Hotels are explicitly nonresidential because transient lodging (stays under 30 days) does not qualify as residential.

The authoritative sources are:
- IRS Publication 946 (How to Depreciate Property)
- IRC §168(e)(2)(A) — defines nonresidential real property
- ASC 360-10 — GAAP property, plant, and equipment guidance

## Depreciable Basis

Not everything can be depreciated. Land never depreciates. The depreciable basis is calculated as:

Depreciable Basis = (Purchase Price × (1 − Land Value %)) + Building Improvements

The land value percentage typically ranges from 15% to 40% depending on location. A downtown property has higher land value than a suburban one.

## Monthly and Annual Depreciation

Monthly Depreciation = Depreciable Basis / 39 / 12
Annual Depreciation = Depreciable Basis / 39

Depreciation starts when the property is placed in service (the acquisition date). No depreciation occurs before that date.

## Tax Impact

Depreciation is a non-cash expense. It reduces taxable income but does not reduce actual cash flow. This is one of the key tax advantages of real estate investing — the depreciation deduction shelters cash income from taxes.

Taxable Income = ANOI − Interest Expense − Depreciation

If taxable income is negative (because depreciation is large enough), no income tax is owed for that period. The resulting Net Operating Loss (NOL) is carried forward under IRC §172 to offset future taxable income.

Income Tax = max(0, Taxable Income) × Income Tax Rate

## Depreciation Recapture on Sale

When a property is sold, the IRS recaptures some of the depreciation benefit under IRC §1250. The gain attributable to prior depreciation deductions is taxed at a maximum rate of 25%, which is higher than the long-term capital gains rate. This is sometimes called "unrecaptured Section 1250 gain."

## Cost Segregation

Cost segregation is an IRS-approved method that accelerates depreciation by reclassifying building components (e.g., FF&E, land improvements, specialty mechanical systems) into shorter MACRS recovery periods (5, 7, or 15 years instead of 39 years). This model does not perform cost segregation analysis but users should be aware it may reduce effective tax burden. A cost segregation study requires a qualified engineer and is most beneficial for properties with a purchase price above $1 million.

## FF&E Depreciation

Furniture, Fixtures, and Equipment (FF&E) are separate from the building and depreciate over a shorter useful life, typically 5 to 7 years. The FF&E reserve set aside each year (usually 3–5% of revenue) funds these replacements. When actual FF&E replacements occur, they are capitalized and depreciated over their useful life.

## Net Book Value

The property's net book value on the balance sheet decreases each year as depreciation accumulates:

Net Book Value = Total Property Cost − Accumulated Depreciation

Where Total Property Cost = Purchase Price + Building Improvements + Pre-Opening Costs.
